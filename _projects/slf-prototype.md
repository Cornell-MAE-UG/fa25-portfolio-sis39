## Client Pitch

### Problem
During mechanical grape harvesting, spotted lanternflies (SLF) are collected along with grape clusters and transported through conveyor systems into storage bins. Even small levels of contamination (as little as 1–2 SLF per 1000 g) can lead to rejected loads and significant economic loss. Because modern harvesters process several tons of grapes per hour, this contamination risk occurs continuously at a large scale. :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}  

Additionally, current post-harvest sorting methods are not effective at this scale. The harvested material consists of a mixture of grapes, stems, juice, and debris, making manual sorting or washing inefficient and potentially resulting in large yield losses. :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}  

---

### Why it matters
Contaminated grape loads reduce product quality and force growers and processors to discard or downgrade harvests, leading to lost revenue. An effective solution must operate inline with existing harvesting systems, maintain high throughput, and avoid damaging grapes. Improving SLF removal would preserve yield, reduce labor-intensive inspection, and allow uninterrupted harvesting. :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}  

---

### Proposed Solution
We proposed an inline mechanical separation system that operates directly on the conveyor during harvesting. The system combines multiple stages of separation, including:
- A rotating bristle roller for initial mechanical removal  
- A compressed air system to dislodge remaining SLF  
- A vacuum system to collect removed insects  

This approach is designed to remove SLF without slowing down the harvesting process or requiring major redesign of existing equipment. :contentReference[oaicite:3]{index=3}  

---

### Key Challenges
Several uncertainties influenced the design:
- High volumetric flow of grapes may shield SLF from removal  
- Grapes and SLF can become compressed together during transport  
- Constraints on modifying existing harvesting equipment  

These challenges guided the development of a simple, modular prototype focused on mechanical separation. :contentReference[oaicite:4]{index=4}

## Functional Prototype

### Purpose
The goal of this prototype was to mechanically remove spotted lanternfly (SLF) models from grape clusters during conveyance without damaging the grapes. The system uses a rotating brush mounted on a shaft to dislodge SLF while allowing grapes to pass through unaffected.

---

### System Overview
The prototype consists of a shaft-mounted strip brush powered by a handheld drill and positioned above a manually operated conveyor surface. As grape clusters move beneath the rotating brush, the bristles apply light contact forces that remove SLF models, which are then directed into a collection trough. :contentReference[oaicite:0]{index=0}  

---

### What was tested
- Brush rotation speed (450–650 RPM range)  
- Bristle coverage across the shaft  
- Conveyor stability during motion  
- Overall SLF removal efficiency  
- Assembly repeatability and structural stability  

---

### Results
- Optimal speed ≈ **500 RPM** for balancing removal and grape stability  
- Average SLF removal ≈ **72%** per pass  
- No visible grape damage observed  
- Some inconsistency in removal due to gaps in brush coverage and conveyor instability :contentReference[oaicite:1]{index=1}  

---

### Outcome
The prototype demonstrates that a rotating brush system can effectively remove SLF during conveyance without damaging grapes. However, performance is limited by uneven bristle coverage and instability in the conveyor surface. Future improvements include increasing bristle density, overlapping brush sections, and reinforcing the conveyor to achieve the target removal efficiency of over 90%. :contentReference[oaicite:2]{index=2}
